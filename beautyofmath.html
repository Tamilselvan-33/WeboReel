01 — The Big Idea

A Matrix transforms space

02 — Area & Orientation

The Determinant is area

The determinant answers: "How much does this transformation scale area?" A det of 2 means areas double. A det of 0 means everything collapses to a line. A negative det means space flips.

💡 KEY INSIGHT — The unit square (area = 1) gets transformed. The blue shaded region shows the NEW area of that square. det = new area / original area. Negative det = space flipped like a page turning over.
+

Positive determinant

Space is stretched. Orientation preserved — what was clockwise stays clockwise. det > 1 = expand. 0 < det < 1 = compress.

−

Negative determinant

Space flips. Like turning a piece of paper over. Orientation reverses — clockwise becomes counter-clockwise.

0

Zero determinant

Space collapses — the entire 2D plane squishes into a line (or a point). You lose a dimension. The matrix has no inverse.

ad−bc

The formula, finally

For matrix [[a,b],[c,d]], det = ad − bc. Now you know WHY — it's the area of the parallelogram formed by the two column vectors.

03 — Composition

Matrix Multiplication = doing transforms one after another

AB doesn't mean "multiply numbers together." It means: first apply B, then apply A. You're composing two transformations into one. Watch the grid go through two steps.

💡 KEY INSIGHT — The order matters! AB ≠ BA in general. Think of it: "rotate then shear" gives a different result than "shear then rotate." Matrix multiplication is reading right to left — the rightmost matrix acts first.
04 — The Special Directions

Eigenvectors don't rotate — they only scale

Most vectors get knocked to a completely different direction when transformed. Eigenvectors are special — they stay on their own line. They just get stretched or squished by a number called the eigenvalue.

💡 KEY INSIGHT — Av = λv. The eigenvector v only gets multiplied by a scalar λ (eigenvalue). λ > 1 = stretched. 0 < λ < 1 = compressed. λ < 0 = flipped. This is why eigenvectors are the "natural axes" of a transformation.
05 — Undoing Transformations

The Inverse undoes everything

If a matrix transforms space one way, its inverse transforms it back. Apply A then A⁻¹ and you're back to where you started. But if det = 0, there's no way back — you can't un-squish a dimension.
